# (WIP) Four Laws of Software Engineering

These are patterns I've seen hold true across every engineering organization I've worked in — from startups to Netflix.  They're not rules to follow, but forces to respect.

## Change is constant

The only constant is change.  There is no static technology, no single thing for engineering to "focus on" and be done.  The platform you built last year is already being outpaced, the framework you chose is already evolving, and the assumptions you made are already being challenged.  Netflix's culture talks about resilience for exactly this reason — the ability to absorb and respond to change is more valuable than any specific technical choice.

AI is proving this law out at an unprecedented pace.  The leap in late 2025 alone — Opus 4, Sonnet 4, GPT 5.2, Gemini 3 Pro — fundamentally shifted what's possible in a matter of months.  Teams that had just finished building their AI strategy had to rethink it.  The rate of change is accelerating, and the organizations that thrive will be the ones that treat change as the default state, not the exception.

## Debt is a forever problem

Tech debt is not a bug to fix, it's a force to manage.  Every system accrues it, and the goal isn't zero debt — it's recognizing that your highest-debt areas are often your ripest opportunities for innovation, and that innovating is one of the best ways to retire debt.  The real trap is treating debt as purely negative; the best teams [reframe it as innovation](tech_debt_as_innovation.md).

## People & Culture Matter

No technical strategy survives a dysfunctional culture.  Psychological safety, selfless leadership, and low ego arbitrage are what allow teams to take risks, share information openly, and hold each other accountable.  Culture isn't a nice-to-have layered on top of execution — it's the organizational infrastructure that makes sustained execution possible.

## Product Success Cures many ills

PMF creates the oxygen that everything else needs.  A team with strong product-market fit can survive imperfect processes, rough edges in the codebase, and organizational growing pains.  Without it, even the best culture and cleanest architecture won't save you.  Get the product right first, then let culture multiply the gains.
