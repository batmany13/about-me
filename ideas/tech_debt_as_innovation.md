# (WIP) Tech Debt as Innovation

_Based on talks at [LeadDev London 2024](https://leaddev.com/technical-direction/tech-debt-as-innovation-reframing-this-forever-problem-as-an-opportunity) and various Enrich/Plato sessions._

## The Origin Story

"Tech Debt as Innovation" started as a marketing campaign.  After talking to several very senior engineers at Netflix, a pattern emerged: nobody cared about tech debt.  It was invisible, thankless work.  But in reality, it was critically important — and it was exactly the kind of work that got these engineers promoted.  The disconnect was in the framing, not the substance.

Rebranding debt work as innovation work changed how executives budgeted for it and how engineers felt about doing it.  Same work, different narrative — and the narrative made all the difference.

## The Core Idea

Tech debt and innovation aren't opposites — they're two sides of the same coin.  The areas of your codebase with the most debt are often the ripest for innovation, and the act of innovating is one of the most effective ways to retire debt.

Instead of treating debt as a backlog to grind through, flip the framing: look at where debt has accumulated the most, and ask what innovation opportunity is hiding there.  A heavily patched legacy service isn't just a liability — it's a signal that the domain is important, well-understood, and ready for a leap forward.

## The Two-Way Street

**Innovation retires debt.** When you build the next-generation system, you don't just add capability — you shed the accumulated compromises of the old one.  The best migrations aren't cleanup projects, they're innovation projects that happen to leave debt behind.

**Debt signals where to innovate.** The highest-debt areas are where the most usage, the most edge cases, and the most institutional knowledge live.  That's exactly where a bold architectural move has the highest leverage.

## The AI Accelerant

AI has fundamentally changed the economics of debt retirement.  What used to require months of careful, manual migration work — updating call sites, refactoring patterns, rewriting tests — can now be done at a pace that was previously impossible.

Large-scale migrations, the kind teams used to defer for years because the effort-to-reward ratio was too painful, are now tractable.  AI can clean up large swaths of code, handle the tedious mechanical parts of migrations, and let engineers focus on the architectural decisions that actually matter.

This means the "tech debt as innovation" flywheel spins faster than ever: you can be bolder about the innovation leap because the cost of cleaning up the transition is dramatically lower.
